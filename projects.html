Alexa Chess

HackEd Hackathon January 2017: In twenty-four hours, myself and four teammates put together a voice-activated and controlled chess game using Amazon's Alexa API. Hosted on a local server, the main framework for the game is Flask, a python-based web server.

Many lessons learned. First, Alexa is not designed for continued interaction, but instead small question-answer blocks of speech. We jumped through many hoops to force the voice recognition process to function continually. Additionally, 24 hours is almost, but not quite, enough time to teach yourself CSS, HTML, and Flask, all at the same time. Revisiting a similar project with my current experience would be significantly easier.

Arbitrary Point-in-Polygon Detector

I was approached by a friend who had a problem at his workplace, and wanted some guidance: given an arbitrary polygon defined by N cartesian vertices, determine whether a point P lies within the polygon. Uses some neat tricks to calculate vector rays without requiring third-party packages.

Why is it important that there are no imports or packages?

Because the poor bastard had to go implement it in VBA, and I felt bad enough already.

Dots and Boxes

Created with Alex Chen, Dots and Boxes is a tkinter implemention of the traditional game of dots and boxes that focuses on efficiently managing the digital representation of the game board. Our version of the game allows for fast, smart decision-making from the AI controlled player, picking a move in worst-case N * M (the square area of the board) time, often making moves in constant time whenever possible

We initially approached the concept as an exercise in creating an AI, but quickly realized that it was more interesting to see how fast we could get it to play against itself.

(Usually it can do 1000 full games in about 1 second on a decent computer, though large boards tank the speed.)
